# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Japan-based leisure travelers planning international flights. They're price-sensitive and comparing codeshare fares before committing to a purchase — the same physical flight sold under different airline codes at different prices. Context is planning mode, not urgency: likely researching over multiple sessions, on desktop or mobile, weighing price against convenience (dates, cabin class, layovers).

## Product Purpose

CoShare.jp detects price gaps between codeshare flights and surfaces the cheapest booking path for the same physical flight. It also supports flexible-date search, annual price trend analysis, and destination recommendations. Success looks like a traveler finding — and trusting — a real savings opportunity in under a minute of searching.

## Brand Personality

Modern, clean, minimal. Understated confidence: quiet typography, generous whitespace, gets out of the way of the data. Trustworthy without being flashy — the price comparison itself is the hero, not the interface around it. Voice is direct and factual (Japanese-first copy, plain and clear), not hypey or sales-y.

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS/AI template look: cream/sand backgrounds, gradient text, eyebrow labels, identical card grids, glassmorphism-as-decoration.
- Cluttered legacy travel sites (Expedia-era): dense layouts, competing CTAs, ad-like visual noise.
- Reference lane: Skyscanner / Google Flights for interaction patterns (clean results list, clear price emphasis, flexible-date affordances) — not for visual skin, which should stay distinctly CoShare's own.

## Design Principles

- Price is the hero. Every results view should make the cheapest/best option immediately scannable; codeshare price gaps must be visually obvious, not buried in fine print.
- Trust through clarity, not decoration. No dark patterns, no artificial urgency — data speaks for itself.
- Quiet by default, precise on demand. Minimal chrome; density and detail (fare rules, codeshare explanation) available on interaction, not forced upfront.
- Japanese-first, bilingual-aware. Typography and layout must handle Japanese text as the primary language gracefully, not as an afterthought translation of an English layout.
- Respect the user's time. Fast perceived performance, clear loading/empty/error states — this is a tool used repeatedly during trip planning, not a one-time landing experience.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA baseline: contrast ratios (4.5:1 body text, 3:1 large text/UI), full keyboard navigation, visible focus states, and `prefers-reduced-motion` alternatives for all motion/animation.
